Medical Debt Court Filings in Massachusetts: WGBH Investigative Reporting

A data storytelling project that translated hidden medical debt lawsuits into actionable insights for investigative newsroom reporting.

Client
Jenifer B. McKim, Investigative Editor at GBH News
Role
Data Analyst (Client Management & Data Storytelling)
Skills
Client Reporting & Executive Presentations, Data Storytelling, Data Visualization, Cross-functional Communication, PostgreSQL, Python

Overview / Description

Medical debt is a persistent financial burden, yet it remains difficult to identify in public court records because lawsuits filed by hospitals and ambulances are typically grouped under general consumer civil cases. GBH News needed to uncover who was aggressively suing patients and how the medical debt landscape was shifting over time.

My role focused heavily on client management and data storytelling. I worked directly with the Investigative Editor to translate their reporting goals into visual narratives and executive presentations, ensuring our technical findings were actionable for a non-technical audience.

Objective / Success Metrics

Surface medical debt lawsuits from large-scale public court records and produce reliable visual narratives and datasets that journalists could directly use to drive their investigative reporting.

Approach

Client Collaboration & Goal Translation

Worked closely with the GBH investigative team to understand their ongoing reporting needs, specifically ensuring that our data pipeline prioritized areas of interest like ambulance and veterinary cases.

Visual Narratives & Executive Presentations

Designed exploratory and presentation-ready charts to surface key trends, such as category shifts and plaintiff concentration. Translated complex SQL/Python pipeline outputs into accessible executive summaries and slide decks.

Data Contextualization

Collaborated with the data engineering team to ensure technical processes (like keyword filtering and RapidFuzz string matching) accurately reflected real-world entities, such as cross-referencing findings with Massachusetts licensed ambulance registries.

Results / Key Findings

  • Refined 3.7 million court records into a targeted primary analysis dataset of approximately 10,500 medical debt-related cases spanning from 2015 to 2025.
  • Illustrated a sharp decline in public court filings for ambulance debt, which fell from more than 1,000 cases in 2015 to fewer than 200 by 2025.
  • Contextualized data trends with journalistic reporting to demonstrate that this decline did not mean the debt disappeared, but rather indicated that debt collection had shifted to private third-party agencies.
  • Highlighted that litigation was highly concentrated among a small number of specific creditors, such as Trinity EMS and MSPCA Angell.
  • Revealed that emergency and urgent care cases often resolved quickly (within 50 to 90 days), providing evidence consistent with high default judgment rates.

Impact / Conclusion / Recommendations

This project demonstrated the power of bridging the gap between raw data engineering and investigative journalism. By focusing on data storytelling and client management, I was able to translate complex data extraction workflows into actionable, systemic insights, directly supporting high-impact reporting on healthcare collection practices.
