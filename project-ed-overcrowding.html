healthcare operations case study

Tackling ED Overcrowding: Optimizing Average Length of Stay and Discharge Workflows

A mixed-methods hospital operations project to improve bed turnover and reduce emergency department congestion.

Duration
Summer 2023 (NYCU Hospital Internship)
Skills
Data Benchmarking, Qualitative Fieldwork, Service Blueprinting, 5W1H Framework, Workflow Optimization

Overview / Description

Emergency Department (ED) overcrowding is a critical global healthcare issue. At NYCU Hospital, the Input-Throughput-Output model showed the primary issue on the output side: patients were stuck in the ED waiting for inpatient beds.

The core business problem was low bed turnover driven by prolonged inpatient average length of stay (ALOS) and delays in the physical discharge process. To relieve ED strain, I investigated both ALOS data patterns and human factors that slowed discharge execution.

Objective / Success Metrics

  1. Part A (Quantitative): Analyze NYCU Hospital's ALOS distribution, benchmark against peer hospitals, and identify departments and disease categories with prolonged stays.
  2. Part B (Qualitative): Map the inpatient discharge process, identify bottlenecks, and propose actionable operational improvements.

Approach

Part A: Data-Driven ALOS Benchmarking

  • Extracted and analyzed admission data from 2018-2023 and cross-referenced National Health Insurance benchmark reports.
  • Found NYCU Hospital's ALOS was consistently longer than peer hospitals, by up to 1.34 days.
  • Department-level analysis identified Internal Medicine and Surgery as the longest-stay departments.
  • Disease-level analysis showed five major categories, including circulatory, digestive, and endocrine diseases, where over 50% of cases exceeded national average ALOS.

Part B: Field Research & Service Blueprinting

  • Conducted a month-long field study across wards, emergency room, and administrative counters.
  • Mapped the end-to-end discharge journey from physician order to final bed cleaning using a service blueprint.
  • Used 5W1H interviews with nurses, doctors, clerks, patients, and cleaning staff to identify hidden bottlenecks.

Results / Key Findings

Using the 5W1H framework, discharge bottlenecks were grouped into three stakeholder areas:

  1. Patient side: Requests for medical certificates and transportation planning were often left until the last minute.
  2. Physician side: Unpredictable ward-round timing delayed discharge orders until late in the day.
  3. Administrative / cleaning side: Clerks had limited control over timelines, and bed-cleaning status was not synchronized with inpatient systems, reducing real-time bed visibility.

Impact / Conclusion / Recommendations

  • Short-term: Set physician KPI expectations so discharge orders are completed within 30 minutes of decision, and publish ward-round schedules to improve communication and discharge readiness.
  • Mid-term: Launch an online portal for certificate requests 2-3 days before discharge, and integrate housekeeping software with the central inpatient system for live clean-bed tracking.
  • Long-term: Strengthen partnerships with nursing homes and home-care providers to reduce ALOS extensions caused by social placement barriers.

This project combined data benchmarking with process engineering to provide hospital administrators a practical roadmap for improving bed turnover and reducing ED congestion.
